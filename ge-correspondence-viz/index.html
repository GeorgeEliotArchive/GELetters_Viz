Interpretation of Results

Across the 3,987 letters in the George Eliot Archive spanning 1836–1881, correspondence patterns reveal a distinctly female-dominant epistolary network. Female-to-female correspondence accounts for the largest single category (1,357 letters, approximately 34% of all identified-gender letters), followed by male-to-male correspondence (983 letters, ~25%). Female senders authored nearly 62% of all letters in the archive, reflecting both George Eliot's prolific personal writing and the broader role of women as primary custodians of social correspondence in the Victorian era.

The chord diagram above visualizes the flow of letters between female and male correspondents. Each arc represents a gender group, sized in proportion to the total volume of letters sent and received. The ribbons connecting the arcs represent the exchange between groups — notably, the ribbon between female senders and male recipients (923 letters) is substantially larger than the reverse (male-to-female: 376 letters), suggesting women more frequently initiated cross-gender correspondence.

The decade-by-decade breakdown reveals a significant surge in correspondence activity during the 1860s and 1870s — periods coinciding with Eliot's peak literary career following the publications of The Mill on the Floss (1860), Middlemarch (1871–72), and Daniel Deronda (1876). Male-to-male letters rise sharply in the 1870s (442 letters), likely driven by the expanding circle of literary, scientific, and intellectual figures engaging with Eliot's household. Cross-gender correspondence (Female→Male: 923; Male→Female: 376) further demonstrates an asymmetry consistent with sociological findings that women tended to initiate and sustain correspondence networks across gender lines more frequently than men during this period. Approximately 348 letters in the full archive contained unidentified or mixed-gender attributions and were excluded from these visualizations.
